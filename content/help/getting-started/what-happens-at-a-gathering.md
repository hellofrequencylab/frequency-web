A gathering is a small group of people doing one thing together for a couple of
hours: a sit, a walk, a workshop, a meal, whatever that Circle is built around.
There is a Host, there is an activity, and there is time to actually talk. That is
it. No performance, no pressure to be interesting, no one putting you on the spot.

## Will it be awkward?

The first five minutes, a little. Then the activity takes over and it gets easy.
This is the part most people dread and it is the part the format is designed to
handle. You are not walking into a room of strangers expected to make small talk
from nothing. There is a thing to do, and the thing gives everyone something to
point at besides each other.

If you are nervous, you are not the only one. Most people in the room felt the
same before their first gathering. Showing up at all is the whole ask.

## What actually happens, start to finish?

A typical gathering runs in three rough parts:

1. **Arrive and settle.** People trickle in, say hello, find a seat. The Host
   usually opens with a quick welcome and maybe a one-line round so everyone has
   said something. Low stakes, short.
2. **The thing itself.** The reason you all came: the practice, the walk, the
   discussion, the project. This is most of the time, and it does the social work
   for you. You are doing something side by side, not facing off.
3. **Wind down.** A little unstructured time at the end to talk, swap numbers if
   you click with someone, and find out when the next one is.

Times vary by Circle, but most run one to two hours.

## What do I need to bring?

Usually nothing. The gathering page lists anything specific (a mat, water, a dish
to share). When in doubt, bring yourself and show up a few minutes early so you
are not walking in late. Some Circles have someone bring oranges or coffee; you do
not have to be that person on day one.

## What if I do not know anyone?

That is the normal starting point, and the Host knows it. A good Host watches for
the new face and makes a point of folding you in. You do not have to introduce
yourself to the whole room or explain why you are there. Stand near the activity,
do the thing, and the conversations happen on their own.

## Questions people ask

### Do I have to talk a lot?

No. You can show up, do the activity, and leave having said very little, and that
counts as a great first gathering. The talking gets easier the second and third
time, when the faces are familiar. Same people, same time, repeated, is how a
hello turns into a friendship.

### How early should I get there?

A few minutes early beats right on time. You get to settle, say hi before things
start, and you are not the person walking in once everyone is seated. If you are
running late, come anyway. Late is better than not at all.

### Can I bring a friend who is not on Frequency?

Yes, and it is a good move. Bringing one person you already know makes the first
five minutes easier for both of you. Share your personal invite link for the
gathering and they can RSVP without an account. See
[how do I RSVP](/help/getting-started/how-to-rsvp).

### Can I leave early if it is not for me?

Yes. Slip out whenever you need to; nobody keeps score. If the Circle is not your
fit, try another one. The point is to find your people, and that sometimes takes a
couple of tries.
